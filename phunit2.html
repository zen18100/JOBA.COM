**Chapter 2: Scalars and Vectors**

Physical quantities in physics are classified as either **scalars** (which only have size) or **vectors** (which require both size and direction). This chapter details the properties and mathematical operations of these quantities.

1. **Scalar Quantities**

**Scalar quantities** are those completely described by a **magnitude** (a number and a unit) and possess **no directional dependence**.

**Clarification:** Scalars follow the rules of ordinary algebra. If you combine two scalar quantities, you just add or subtract their magnitudes. For example, 1 hour plus 2 hours equals 3 hours, always.

Examples of Scalars

  • **Mass** (e.g., 5 kg)
  • **Time** (e.g., 30 seconds)
  • **Distance** (e.g., 10 meters)
  • **Temperature**, **Speed**, **Energy**, **Electric Current**

2. **Vector Quantities**

**Vector quantities** are defined by both a **magnitude** and a **direction**.

**Clarification:** Because direction matters, vectors must be added and subtracted using special geometric and trigonometric rules (Vector Algebra). For example, a 10 N force plus another 10 N force can result in anything from 0 N to 20 N, depending on the angle between them.

Examples of Vectors

  • **Force** (e.g., 100 N pulling North)
  • **Velocity** (e.g., 15 m/s moving at 45° East of North)
  • **Acceleration**, **Displacement**, **Momentum**, **Electric Field Intensity**

3. **Representation and Definitions**

3.1 Vector Notation

Vectors are identified by their notation, which distinguishes them from scalars:

  • **Printed Text:** A vector is represented by a **bold letter**, such as **A** or **r**.
  • **Handwriting & Formulas:** A vector is typically written as a letter followed by a right-arrow (e.g., A→ or r→), which will be used in the formulas below.
  • **Magnitude:** The magnitude of a vector A→ is its length, written as |A→| or simply A (not bold).

3.2 Graphical Representation

A vector is drawn as an **arrow**. The **length** of the arrow represents the **magnitude** (to a chosen scale), and the **arrowhead** indicates the **direction**.

3.3 Vector Definitions

  • **Equal Vectors:** Two vectors, A→ and B→, are equal if they have the **same magnitude** and point in the **same direction**.
  • **Negative Vector:** The negative of a vector A→ (written as -A→) has the **same magnitude** as A→ but points in the **opposite direction**.
  • **Unit Vector (n→):** A dimensionless vector with a **magnitude of exactly one (1)**, used purely to specify a direction. The unit vector along A→ is:
    n→ = A→ / |A→|
  • **Zero Vector (Null Vector):** A vector whose magnitude is **zero**. Its direction is undefined.

4. **Vector Addition (Geometric & Analytical)**

The result of adding two or more vectors is called the **resultant vector** (R→).

4.1 **Triangle Law of Addition (Geometric)**

Used to find the resultant R→ = A→ + B→ graphically:

  • **Step 1:** Draw vector A→ to scale.
  • **Step 2:** Place the **tail** of vector B→ at the **head** (tip) of vector A→ (Tip-to-Tail Rule).
  • **Step 3:** The **resultant R→** is the vector drawn from the **tail of A→** to the **head of B→**.

Magnitude Calculation (Law of Cosines)

If A and B are placed tail-to-tail, and ∅ is the angle between them, the magnitude of the resultant R is given by:

R = |R→| = √(A² + B² + 2AB cos(∅))

4.2 **Vector Subtraction**

Vector subtraction (R→ = A→ - B→) is achieved by finding the negative vector -B→ and adding it to A→: R→ = A→ + (-B→).

4.3 **Analytical Method (Components)**

This is the most precise method, relying on breaking down vectors into perpendicular components along coordinate axes, defined by unit vectors i→ (x-axis), j→ (y-axis), and k→ (z-axis).

Vector Resolution

Any vector A→ can be written in component form (3D):

A→ = Aₓ i→ + Aᵧ j→ + A𝓏 k→

Where Aₓ = A cos(∅) and Aᵧ = A sin(∅) for a 2D vector making angle ∅ with the positive x-axis.

Addition Procedure

  • **Step 1:** Sum the corresponding components of all vectors to find the resultant components Rₓ, Rᵧ, R𝓏:
    • Rₓ = Aₓ + Bₓ + Cₓ + ...
    Rᵧ = Aᵧ + Bᵧ + Cᵧ + ...
    R𝓏 = A𝓏 + B𝓏 + C𝓏 + ...
  • **Step 2:** Calculate the **Magnitude (R)**:
    • R = |R→| = √(Rₓ² + Rᵧ² + R𝓏²)
  • **Step 3 (2D only):** Find the **Direction (∅)** of the resultant:
    • ∅ = tan⁻¹(Rᵧ / Rₓ)

Example: Resultant Velocity

Calculate the resultant velocity R→ = V₁→ + V₂→.

Given: V₁→ = 6 i→ + 4 j→ (m/s) and V₂→ = -2 i→ + 8 j→ (m/s)

  • **Find Resultant Components (Rₓ, Rᵧ):**
    • Rₓ = 6 + (-2) = 4 m/s
    Rᵧ = 4 + 8 = 12 m/s
    Resultant Vector: R→ = 4 i→ + 12 j→ (m/s)
  • **Find Magnitude (R):**
    • R = √(4² + 12²) = √(16 + 144) = √160 ≈ 12.65 m/s
  • **Find Direction (∅):**
    • ∅ = tan⁻¹(12 / 4) = tan⁻¹(3) ≈ 71.6°

**Result:** The resultant velocity is **12.65 m/s at 71.6°** above the positive x-axis.

5. **Multiplying Vectors**

There are two primary ways to multiply vectors, yielding either a scalar or another vector.

5.1 **The Scalar (Dot) Product**

The **dot product** (A→ ⋅ B→) results in a **scalar** quantity. It measures how much of one vector points in the direction of the other.

Formula and Properties

  • **Definition (Magnitude and Angle):** ∅ is the angle between A→ and B→.
    • A→ ⋅ B→ = A B cos(∅)
  • **Component Form:** Sum the products of corresponding components.
    • A→ ⋅ B→ = AₓBₓ + AᵧBᵧ + A𝓏B𝓏
  • **Orthogonality Test:** If A→ and B→ are **perpendicular** (∅ = 90°), their dot product is 0.

Example: Dot Product Calculation

Find the dot product of A→ = 2 i→ + 1 j→ and B→ = 4 i→ - 8 j→.

A→ ⋅ B→ = (2)(4) + (1)(-8)
A→ ⋅ B→ = 8 - 8 = **0**

Since the dot product is zero, the vectors A→ and B→ are perpendicular to each other.

5.2 **The Vector (Cross) Product**

The **cross product** (A→ $\times$ B→) results in a new **vector** (C→). The resultant vector C→ is always **perpendicular** to the plane formed by A→ and B→.

Formula and Properties

  • **Definition (Magnitude):** ∅ is the angle between A→ and B→.
    • |C→| = |A→ $\times$ B→| = A B sin(∅)
  • **Direction:** Determined by the **Right-Hand Rule**.
  • **Non-Commutative:** The order is critical: A→ $\times$ B→ = - (B→ $\times$ A→).
  • **Parallelism Test:** If A→ and B→ are **parallel** (∅ = 0°), their cross product is the zero vector (0→).

Component Formula

If C→ = A→ $\times$ B→, the components of C→ are calculated using the following expansion:

Cₓ = (AᵧB𝓏 - A𝓏Bᵧ)
Cᵧ = (A𝓏Bₓ - AₓB𝓏)
C𝓏 = (AₓBᵧ - AᵧBₓ)

Example: Cross Product Calculation

Find the cross product C→ = A→ $\times$ B→.

Given: A→ = 1 i→ + 2 j→ + 3 k→ and B→ = 4 i→ + 5 j→ + 6 k→

  • **Calculate Cₓ (i component):**
    • Cₓ = (AᵧB𝓏 - A𝓏Bᵧ) = (2)(6) - (3)(5) = 12 - 15 = -3
  • **Calculate Cᵧ (j component):**
    • Cᵧ = (A𝓏Bₓ - AₓB𝓏) = (3)(4) - (1)(6) = 12 - 6 = 6
  • **Calculate C𝓏 (k component):**
    • C𝓏 = (AₓBᵧ - AᵧBₓ) = (1)(5) - (2)(4) = 5 - 8 = -3

**Result:** C→ = A→ $\times$ B→ = **-3 i→ + 6 j→ - 3 k→**
