1. Pressure and Properties of Gases

A gas is a substance that is normally in the gaseous state at room temperature and pressure (rtp). The term vapour refers to a gas formed by evaporation of a liquid or sublimation of a solid.

1.1 Properties of Gases

  • Are readily compressible.
  • Completely fill any container and as a result have a low density.
  • Mix completely with any other gas.
  • Exert pressure on their surroundings.

Unlike condensed phases, gases occupy volumes that depend on pressure (P) and temperature (T). Standard temperature and pressure (STP) refers to 0°C (273.15 K) and 1 atm. Room temperature and pressure (RTP) refers to 25°C and 1 atm. At STP, 1 mole of an ideal gas occupies 22.4 L.

1.2 Definition of Pressure and Units

Pressure is defined as force per unit area.

1 atm = 760 mm Hg = 760 Torr = 101325 Pa (or N/m²)

The pressure exerted by a column of fluid is calculated as:

P (in Pa) = h × d × g

Where h is column height, d is fluid density, and g is acceleration due to gravity. Manometers measure pressure differences, while barometers measure atmospheric pressure.

2. The Gas Laws

When a gas obeys these laws, it behaves as an ideal gas or a perfect gas.

2.1 Boyle's Law

The pressure of a fixed amount of gas is inversely proportional to its volume at constant temperature.

V ∝ 1/P   ⇒   P1V1 = P2V2

2.2 Charles' Law

The volume of a fixed amount of gas at constant pressure is directly proportional to its absolute temperature in Kelvin.

V ∝ T   ⇒   V1/T1 = V2/T2

2.3 Gay-Lussac's Law

Provided volume is constant, the pressure of a fixed amount of gas varies directly with temperature.

P ∝ T   ⇒   P1/T1 = P2/T2

2.4 Combined Gas Law

A combination of Boyle's, Charles', and Gay-Lussac's Laws.

(P1V1) / T1 = (P2V2) / T2

2.5 Avogadro's Law

Provided temperature and pressure are fixed, equal volumes of all gases contain the same number of molecules.

V ∝ n   ⇒   V1/n1 = V2/n2

2.6 The Ideal Gas Law

An ideal gas exhibits no attractive/repulsive forces and the volume of the molecules is negligibly small compared to the container.

PV = nRT
  • R = 0.08206 (L·atm)/(K·mol) when V is in Litres and P is in atm.
  • R = 8.314 J/(K·mol) when using SI units (m³ and Pa).

3. Gas Stoichiometry

3.1 Gas Density and Molar Mass

Using the density formula d = m / V and the ideal gas law, we can derive equations to find the density or molar mass of a gas.

PMr = dRT

Where Mr is the molar mass of the gas.

Example Problem: Density at STP

A mole of gas at 450 K has a density of 1.41 g/dm³ at 2.0 atm. What is the density at STP?

P1 / (T1 · d1) = P2 / (T2 · d2) d2 = (T1 · d1 · P2) / (T2 · P1) d2 = (450 × 1.41 × 1.0) / (273 × 2) = 1.16 g/dm³

4. Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

For a mixture of gases in a container, the total pressure exerted is the sum of the pressures that each gas would exert if it were alone. It is the total number of moles of particles that is important, not the identity of the gas.

Ptotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...

4.1 Mole Fraction (x)

The ratio of the number of moles of a given component to the total number of moles in the mixture.

x1 = n1 / ntotal = P1 / Ptotal

The sum of all mole fractions in a mixture equals 1.

5. Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT) of Gases

This theory models ideal gas behavior based on particles in motion.

5.1 Postulates of KMT

  1. Particles are so small that the volume of the individual particles is assumed to be negligible (zero).
  2. Particles are in constant motion. Collisions with the walls cause the pressure exerted by the gas.
  3. Particles are assumed to exert no forces on each other (neither attract nor repel).
  4. The average kinetic energy of a collection of gas particles is directly proportional to the Kelvin temperature.

These postulates perfectly explain all gas laws. For example, decreasing volume increases collision frequency, leading to higher pressure (Boyle's Law).

6. Effusion and Diffusion

6.1 Effusion (Graham's Law)

Effusion is the movement of a gas through a small opening into a vacuum. Thomas Graham's Law states that the rate of effusion is inversely proportional to the square root of the molar mass of the gas particles. Lighter gases effuse faster.

Rate1 / Rate2 = √(M2 / M1) = √(d2 / d1)

6.2 Diffusion

Diffusion is the gradual mixing of molecules of one gas with molecules of another by virtue of their kinetic properties. It proceeds from a region of higher concentration to lower concentration.

7. Real Gases and Deviations

No gas exactly follows the ideal gas law. Real gases behave most ideally at low pressures and high temperatures. At high pressures, molecules are forced close together (volume is no longer negligible), and at low temperatures, intermolecular forces become significant.

7.1 The Van der Waals Equation

Johannes van der Waals modified the ideal gas law to correct for finite molecular volume and attractive forces between molecules.

[ Pobs + a(n/V)² ] × [ V - nb ] = nRT
  • Correction for Volume (V - nb): Subtracts the volume occupied by the gas molecules themselves. b is a constant specific to the gas.
  • Correction for Pressure [ P + a(n/V)² ]: Accounts for attractive forces between gas particles, which reduces the observed pressure. a is a constant specific to the gas.
