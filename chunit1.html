1. The Scientific Method

The Scientific Method is a systematic, logical approach to research.

1.1 Steps of the Method

  1. Define a problem: Notice something interesting.

  2. Experiment: Design and perform an investigation to test the hypothesis. Experiments must be controlled and must produce repeatable data.

  3. Observation: Notice what is happening in the experiment and collect data.

    The data collected can be Quantitative (Measurable) or Qualitative (properties)

  4. Hypothesis: this is a testable, possible explanation to an observation.

  5. Law: is mathematical statement of a relationship between phenomena that is always the same under the same conditions
  6. Theory: This is a set of tested hypothesis that gives an overall explanation of natural phenomina

2. Dalton's Atomic Theory

John Dalton's theory was the first comprehensive model describing matter as being composed of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms. It is the basis of modern chemistry.

2.1 The Four Main Postulates

  • Matter is made of Atoms: All matter is composed of extremely small, indivisible particles called atoms. (Modern change: Atoms are divisible into subatomic particles.)
  • Atoms of the Same Element are Identical: All atoms of a given element are identical in size, mass, and other properties; atoms of different elements differ in these properties. (Modern change: Isotopes exist, which are atoms of the same element with different masses.)
  • Atoms Cannot Be Created or Destroyed: Atoms cannot be created or destroyed, nor can they be converted into atoms of other elements. Chemical reactions simply rearrange how atoms are grouped. (This explains the Law of Conservation of Mass.)
  • Compounds are Formed by Combining Atoms: Atoms of different elements combine in simple whole-number ratios to form chemical compounds. (This explains the Law of Definite Proportions and the Law of Multiple Proportions.)

Key Laws Explained by Dalton

Dalton's theory successfully explained the foundational laws of chemistry:

  • Law of Conservation of Mass: Since atoms are not created or destroyed in a reaction (Postulate 3), the total mass of the system remains constant.
  • Law of Definite Proportions: Any compound is formed by specific atoms combined in a fixed ratio (Postulate 4). For example, water is always 8 parts oxygen to 1 part hydrogen by mass.

3. Classification and Properties of Matter

3.1 Classification of Matter

  • Substances: This is a form of matter with fixed composition and properties (Elements and Compounds).
    • Element: This is a substance that cannot be separated into simpler substances by chemical means.
    • Compound This is a substance composed of two or more elements chemically united in fixed proportions.
  • Mixtures: This is a form of matter with variable composition; components retain their properties.
    • Homogeneous: Uniform mixture (e.g., saltwater).
    • Heterogeneous: Non-uniform mixture (e.g., sand and water).

3.2 Properties and Changes

  • Physical Property/Change: Can be observed without changing identity (e.g., melting, boiling, density).
  • Chemical Property/Change: Describes how a substance changes into a new substance (e.g., flammability, rusting, burning).
  • Extensive Property: Depends on the amount of matter (e.g., mass, volume).
  • Intensive Property: Independent of the amount of matter (e.g., density, temperature, melting point).

4.2 Derived Units

  • Volume: Derived from length cubed (m³). Common in lab: Litre (L), where 1L = 1dm³ and 1ml = 1cm³.
  • Density: Mass per unit volume mass/volume. Units: g/ml or g/cm³.

4.3 Conversions

You arrange the conversion factor(s) so that the unwanted (starting) unit cancels out, leaving only the desired (final) unit.

Starting Quantity × Desired Unit ÷ Starting Unit = Final Quantity
  1. Identify the starting unit and the desired final unit.
  2. Find the conversion factors that connect the starting unit to the desired unit.
  3. Set up the calculation so that all units except the desired unit cancel out (starting unit must be in the denominator of the conversion factor).
  4. Perform the multiplication and division.

Example Problem (Dimensional Analysis)

How many meters are in 3.5 miles? (Use the conversion: 1 mile = 1.609 km and 1 km = 1000 m)

  • We need to convert miles to kilometers (km) to meters (m).

    • 3.5miles × 1.609 km ÷ 1 mile × 1000 m ÷ 1 km

The result is 5631.5 m. (Notice how miles and km units cancel out.)
