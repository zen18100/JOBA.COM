1. Water, The Common Solvent

Much of the chemistry that affects each of us occurs among substances dissolved in water.

1.1 Structure of Water

  • Oxygen's electronegativity is high (3.5) and hydrogen's is low (2.1).
  • Water is a bent molecule.
  • Water is a polar molecule, possessing a positive end and a negative end.

1.2 Hydration of Solutes

  • Ionic Solute Molecules: Positive ions are attracted to the oxygen end of water, while negative ions are attracted to the hydrogen end.
  • Polar Solute Molecules: The negative end of a polar solute is attracted to water's hydrogen, and the positive end is attracted to water's oxygen.
  • "Like Dissolves Like": Polar and ionic compounds dissolve in polar solvents like water. Nonpolar compounds like fats dissolve in nonpolar solvents like petrol.

2. Strong and Weak Electrolytes

An electrolyte is a substance that when dissolved in water produces a solution that can conduct an electric current.

2.1 Types of Electrolytes

  • Strong Electrolytes: Conduct current very efficiently because they are completely ionized in water.
    • Ionic compounds / salts (e.g., NaCl(aq))
    • Strong acids (e.g., HNO3, H2SO4, HCl)
    • Strong bases (e.g., NaOH, KOH)
  • Weak Electrolytes: Conduct only a small current because they are slightly ionized in solution.
    • Weak acids (e.g., organic acids like acetic, citric, butyric)
    • Weak bases (e.g., ammonia)
  • Nonelectrolytes: Conduct no current because there are no ions present in solution (e.g., alcohols, sugars).

3. The Composition of Solutions

To perform stoichiometric calculations, we must know the exact forms the chemicals take when dissolved and the amounts of chemicals present (concentrations).

3.1 Molarity

Molarity (M) is defined as moles of solute per volume of solution in liters.

Molarity (M) = Moles of solute (n) / Litres of solution (V)

Example Problem: Finding Molarity

Calculate the concentration in moles per liter of a solution of calcium chloride made by dissolving 27g of calcium chloride to make 500ml of solution.

  • Find the molar mass of CaCl2: 40.08 + 2(35.45) = 110.98 g/mol.
  • Find moles (n): n = 27 g / 110.98 g/mol = 0.24 mol.
  • Convert volume to Litres: V = 500 ml = 0.500 L.
  • Calculate Molarity: M = 0.24 mol / 0.500 L = 0.48 mol/L.

3.2 Concentration of Ions and Dilution

  • Concentration of Ions: Ionic compounds dissociate in solution. Multiply the solution's molarity by the subscript value of the ion in the formula.
  • Moles from Concentration: You can find moles using n = V × M.
  • Dilution: Diluting a solution with water does not change the number of moles present. Use the formula:
    M1V1 = M2V2

4. Types of Chemical Reactions

  • Precipitation Reactions: When two solutions are mixed, an insoluble solid forms.
  • Acid-Base Reactions: A soluble hydroxide and a soluble acid react to form water and a salt.
  • Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions: Reactions in which one or more electrons are transferred.

5. Precipitation Reactions

Ionic compounds dissolve in water, and the ions separate and move independently. To determine products, you "switch partners" of the reactant ions.

5.1 Determining the Precipitate

For example, reacting AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq): The possible products are AgCl and NaNO3. Based on solubility rules, AgCl is insoluble, so it forms a white precipitate. If there is no insoluble product, no reaction occurs.

5.2 Solubility Rules

  1. Most nitrate (NO3-) salts are soluble.
  2. Most salts containing alkali metal ions (Li+, Na+, K+) and the ammonium (NH4+) ion are soluble.
  3. Most chloride, bromide, and iodide salts are soluble, except those containing Ag+, Pb2+, and Hg2+.
  4. Most sulphate salts are soluble, except BaSO4, PbSO4, HgSO4, and CaSO4.
  5. Most hydroxide salts are slightly soluble (NaOH and KOH are soluble; Ba(OH)2, Sr(OH)2, and Ca(OH)2 are marginally soluble).
  6. Most sulphide, carbonate, chromate, and phosphate salts are only slightly soluble.

6. Describing Reactions in Solution

  • Molecular Equation: Gives the overall reaction stoichiometry.
    Na2CO3(aq) + Ca(NO3)2(aq) → 2NaNO3(aq) + CaCO3(s)
  • Complete Ionic Equation: Represents all strong electrolytes as separated ions.
    2Na+(aq) + CO32-(aq) + Ca2+(aq) + 2NO3-(aq) → 2Na+(aq) + 2NO3-(aq) + CaCO3(s)
  • Net Ionic Equation: Includes only components that take part in the chemical change. Spectator ions are eliminated.
    Ca2+(aq) + CO32-(aq) → CaCO3(s)

7. Acid-Base (Neutralization) Reactions

7.1 Definitions

  • Bronsted: Acids are proton donors, bases are proton acceptors.
  • Arrhenius: Acids produce H+ in water, bases produce OH- ions in solution.

The net ionic equation for a strong acid-base reaction is usually: H+(aq) + OH-(aq) → H2O(l)

7.2 Acid-Base Titrations

  • Titrant: Solution of known concentration.
  • Analyte: Solution of unknown concentration.
  • Equivalence Point: Point at which the amount of titrant added results in perfect neutralization.
  • Indicator: A substance that changes color at the equivalence point.
  • Endpoint: The actual point at which the indicator changes color.

8. Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions

Oxidation-reduction reactions are characterized by a transfer of electrons.

  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons, increase in oxidation state. This substance is the reducing agent.
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons, decrease in oxidation state. This substance is the oxidizing agent.
  • Mnemonic: LEO (Lose Electrons = Oxidation) says GER (Gain Electrons = Reduction).

8.1 Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

Rule Summary
The oxidation number of an atom of a free element is zero.Element = 0
The oxidation number of a monatomic ion equals its charge.Ion = Charge
In compounds, oxygen is -2, except in peroxides where it is -1.Oxygen = -2
In compounds, hydrogen has an oxidation number of +1.Hydrogen = +1
In compounds, fluorine is ALWAYS assigned -1.Fluorine = -1
The sum of the oxidation states for an electrically neutral compound must be zero.Sum = 0

Example: Combustion of methane: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g). Carbon changes from -4 to +4 (oxidation), and oxygen changes from 0 to -2 (reduction). CH4 is the reducing agent, and O2 is the oxidizing agent.

9. Balancing Oxidation-Reduction Equations

9.1 Ion-Electron (Half-Reaction) Method

This method is preferred for complex reactions (like those involving oxo anions such as Cr2O72- or MnO4-).

Steps for Balancing in Acidic Medium

  • Step 1: Write the unbalanced equation in ion form.
  • Step 2: Separate the equation into an oxidation half-reaction and a reduction half-reaction.
  • Step 3: Balance all atoms other than O and H.
  • Step 4: Add H2O to balance O atoms. Then add H+ ions to balance H atoms.
  • Step 5: Add electrons (e-) to one side of each half-reaction to balance the charges. Multiply half-reactions by integers to equalize the number of electrons.
  • Step 6: Add the half-reactions together and cancel identical species (including electrons).
  • Step 7: Verify that atoms and charges are balanced.

Note for Basic Solutions: Follow all the steps above as if it were acidic. Then, for every H+ ion present in the final equation, add an equal number of OH- ions to both sides. Combine H+ and OH- on the same side to form H2O, and cancel any redundant H2O molecules.
