Units and Measurements

Physics is the study of changes in the universe based on measurement. A measurement involves comparing a physical quantity (like length or time) to a standardized reference known as a unit. The result is a numerical magnitude which indicates how many times the unit fits into the quantity.

1. Types of Quantities & Units

1.1 Fundamental Quantities

A fundamental quantity is a physical quantity that is considered independent of all other quantities. It cannot be defined using any other physical quantity. Its unit is called a fundamental unit.

For example, you cannot define mass using only length and time. In the field of mechanics, the three core fundamental units are Length (m), Mass (kg), and Time (s).

1.2 Derived Quantities

A derived quantity is any physical quantity that is formed by the mathematical combination (multiplication, division, etc.) of two or more fundamental quantities. Its unit is called a derived unit.

Example: Velocity and Force

  • Velocity: Defined as distance (Length) / time. Unit: m/s.
  • Force: Defined as mass × acceleration (acceleration is length / time²). Unit: kg·m/s², which is specially named the Newton (N).

3. Unit Conversion & Standards

3.1 The Chain-Link Conversion Method (Factor-Label Method)

This method uses a conversion factor (a fraction equal to 1) to change the units of a quantity without changing its value. The unit you want to eliminate must be placed in the denominator of the conversion factor so it cancels algebraically.

Step-by-Step Example: Convert 25.0 meters per second (m/s) to kilometers per hour (km/h).

  • Step 1: Convert meters (m) to kilometers (km). Use the factor (1km / 1000m).
  • Step 2: Convert seconds (s) to hours (h). We know 1h = 60min and 1min = 60s, so 1h = 3600s. Use the factor (3600s / 1h).
  • Step 3: Set up the calculation and cancel units:
25.0m/s × (1km / 1000m) × (3600s / 1h)
= (25.0 × 3600) / 1000 km/h
= 90 km/h

3.2 Official Standards

The definitions of the fundamental SI units are based on intrinsic properties (built-in charecteristics) of nature, guaranteeing they are invariant (do not change) and precise:

  • Meter (Length): Defined via the constant speed of light.
  • Second (Time): Defined via the fixed frequency of radiation emitted by a Cesium-133 atom.
  • Kilogram (Mass): Defined via the fixed value of the Planck constant (h).

4. Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is a technique to check the consistency of equations and derive relationships between physical quantities using their fundamental dimensions: Mass [M], Length [L], and Time [T].

4.1 Principle of Homogeneity

This principle is a core rule: all terms in a valid physical equation must have the exact same dimensions. This ensures that the calculation is meaningful (e.g., you cannot add a time to a length).

Step-by-Step Example: Deriving a Formula

The time period (T) of an oscillating spring depends on mass (m) and spring constant (k). Find the formula form, assuming T = kᵃ mᵇ.

Spring Constant (k) is defined by Force / displacement: [MLT⁻² / L] = [MT⁻²].

  • Step 1: Write the dimensional equation by substituting dimensions:
    • [T] = [MT⁻²]ᵃ × [M]ᵇ = [Mᵃ⁺ᵇ T⁻²ᵃ]
  • Step 2: Equate the powers of M and T on both sides:
    • For M: 0 = a + b (Equation 1)
    For T: 1 = -2a (Equation 2)
  • Step 3: Solve the system of equations:
    • From (2), a = -1/2
    Substitute a into (1): 0 = (-1/2) + b ⇒ b = 1/2
  • Step 4: Substitute the powers back into the original formula:
    • T = (k)⁻¹⁄² (m)¹⁄² = m¹⁄² / k¹⁄²
    T is proportional to the square root of (m/k).

5. Significant Figures & Rounding Off

5.1 Rules for Identifying Significant Figures (Sig Figs)

Sig figs represent the certainty of a measurement. You count them from the first non-zero digit on the left.

  • Rule 1 (Non-zeros): Always significant (e.g., 123 has 3 sig figs).
  • Rule 2 (Sandwich Zeros): Zeros between non-zeros are significant (e.g., 102 has 3 sig figs).
  • Rule 3 (Leading Zeros): Zeros used as place holders are not significant including the first zero(e.g., 0.0078 has 2 sig figs).
  • Rule 4 (Trailing Zeros): Zeros on the right end are significant only if a decimal point is explicitly present (e.g., 400. has 3 sig figs while 400 has 1 sig fig).

5.2 Calculation Rules (Rounding Off)

When calculating a result, you must round the final answer so its precision reflects the least precise input measurement.

  • Addition/Subtraction: The result must be rounded to have the same number of decimal places as the measurement with the fewest decimal places.
  • Multiplication/Division: The result must be rounded to have the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest significant figures.

6. Errors in Measurement & Propagation

6.1 Calculating Error Components

A measured value A is reported as: (Mean Value) ± (Absolute Error), or A ± ΔA.

  • Absolute Error (ΔA): The uncertainty or variation in a measurement, measured in the same units as A.
  • Relative (Fractional) Error: A dimensionless measure of precision, calculated as the ratio of absolute error to the mean value: ΔA / A.
  • Percent Error: The fractional error multiplied by 100%: (ΔA / A) × 100%.

6.2 Combination (Propagation) of Errors

When multiple uncertain measurements (A, B) are combined mathematically to find a result X, their individual errors (ΔA, ΔB) propagate to determine the final error (ΔX).

Formula Summary

Function Error Formula Description
Sum or Difference
X = A + B or X = A - B		 ΔX = ± (ΔA + ΔB) The maximum absolute errors are added directly.
Product or Quotient
X = A × B or X = A / B		 ΔX / X = ± (ΔA / A + ΔB / B) The maximum fractional (relative) errors are added.
Power
X = Aⁿ		 ΔX / X = ± |n|(ΔA / A) The fractional error is multiplied by the magnitude of the power (n).

Example 1: Addition/Subtraction (Absolute Error Addition)

Calculate the total resistance R = R₁ + R₂.

Given: R₁ = 10.5Ω ± 0.5Ω and R₂ = 5.2Ω ± 0.3Ω.

  • Step 1: Calculate the mean value of R:
    • R = R₁ + R₂ = 10.5Ω + 5.2Ω = 15.7Ω
  • Step 2: Calculate the absolute error (ΔR) by adding the individual absolute errors:
    • ΔR = ΔR₁ + ΔR₂ = 0.5Ω + 0.3Ω = 0.8Ω
  • Result: The total resistance is 15.7Ω ± 0.8Ω.

Example 2: Multiplication/Division (Fractional Error Addition)

Calculate the Density (ρ) = Mass / Volume.

Given: Mass (M) = 20g ± 1g and Volume (V) = 5cm³ ± 0.1cm³.

  • Step 1: Calculate the mean value of Density (ρ):
    • ρ = M / V = 20g / 5cm³ = 4 g/cm³
  • Step 2: Calculate the fractional error for each quantity:
    • ΔM / M = 1 / 20 = 0.05
    ΔV / V = 0.1 / 5 = 0.02
  • Step 3: Calculate the fractional error for Density (Δρ / ρ) by adding the fractional errors:
    • Δρ / ρ = (ΔM / M) + (ΔV / V) = 0.05 + 0.02 = 0.07
  • Step 4: Convert the total fractional error back to an absolute error (Δρ):
    • Δρ = 0.07 × ρ = 0.07 × 4 g/cm³ = 0.28 g/cm³
  • Result: The Density is 4 g/cm³ ± 0.28 g/cm³.

Example 3: Powers (Exponent Multiplies Fractional Error)

Calculate the Area (A) = L² of a square.

Given: Length (L) = 5.0m ± 0.2m.

  • Step 1: Calculate the mean value of Area (A):
    • A = (5.0m)² = 25m²
  • Step 2: Calculate the fractional error for Length (ΔL / L):
    • ΔL / L = 0.2 / 5.0 = 0.04
  • Step 3: Calculate the fractional error for Area (ΔA / A) by multiplying the fractional error by the power (n=2):
    • ΔA / A = 2 × (ΔL / L) = 2 × 0.04 = 0.08
  • Step 4: Calculate the absolute error (ΔA):
    • ΔA = 0.08 × A = 0.08 × 25m² = 2m²
  • Result: The Area is 25m² ± 2m².

7. Density and Atomic Mass

Density (ρ) is an intrinsic property of a substance, which means it doesn't depend on the amount of substance present. It relates the mass of a body to the volume it occupies:

Density (ρ) = Mass / Volume

7.1 Atomic Mass and Moles

The atomic mass unit (u) is used to measure the mass of individual atoms. The concept of the mole links the atomic scale to the macroscopic scale.

  • Atomic Mass Unit: 1u is defined as 1/12th the mass of a single Carbon-12 atom. 1u = 1.66 × 10⁻²⁷kg.
  • Avogadro's Number (Nₐ): This is the number of particles (atoms or molecules) in one mole of any substance.
Nₐ = 6.02 × 10²³ molecules/mole

8. Order of Magnitude

The order of magnitude is the power of 10 that best approximates the size of a quantity. It is used for quick estimation and comparison of very large or very small numbers.

Step-by-Step for Finding the Order of Magnitude:

  • Step 1: Express the quantity in standard scientific notation: A × 10ᵇ, where A is between 1 and 10.
  • Step 2: Check the coefficient A:
    • If A is less than 5 (A < 5), the order of magnitude is 10ᵇ.
    • If A is 5 or greater (A ≥ 5), the order of magnitude is 10ᵇ⁺¹ (round up the exponent).

Estimation Examples

1. Quantity: 3.8 × 10⁴. Coefficient (3.8) < 5. Order of Magnitude: 10⁴.

2. Quantity: 7.5 × 10⁻¹⁵. Coefficient (7.5) ≥ 5. Order of Magnitude: 10⁻¹⁵⁺¹ = 10⁻¹⁴.
